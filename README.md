# The Sandwich Trap
### A Formal Model of Structural Poverty in Temporal Forge

---

## What This Is

A person is born. They want a sandwich. The sandwich represents stability ‚Äî a job, housing, a future, dignity. Three avenues can give them one: get a job, ask a stranger, or apply for government aid.

Each avenue has a catch. Each catch is not arbitrary ‚Äî it is generated by the exact condition that poverty itself produces. The model traces a full life, from birth to elder age, and formally proves that for certain starting configurations, no sequence of actions across any number of time steps can ever reach the sandwich.

This is not a simulation of bad luck. It is a proof that the system is designed to produce this outcome.

## The Big Idea

The model is built around **catch-22 structures**: situations where escaping condition A requires first having condition B, but acquiring condition B requires first escaping condition A. These are not logical paradoxes. They are the actual design of bureaucratic and economic systems. The person is not failing to try hard enough. They are trapped in a formally closed loop.

The model adds a second layer: **misreporting**. The media and employer background check systems treat survival infractions (sitting in a park while homeless) as morally and factually equivalent to violent felonies (armed robbery, murder). A criminal record is a criminal record. The system cannot or will not distinguish between them, and the media actively pools them into the same news story. This transforms a survival behavior into a permanent disqualifier, and that disqualification generates more survival behaviors, which generate more disqualifiers.

In Forge terms: the trap is **UNSAT**. There is no satisfying assignment of steps that leads from the starting state to the goal state. The solver is not finding that the goal is hard to reach. It is proving the goal is unreachable.

## The Three Original Avenues

### Avenue 1: Get a Job
A person can earn a sandwich by being employed, having an address for paperwork, presenting a professional appearance, having no resume gap, and passing an employer background check.

The catch: the background check screens out any criminal record. Criminal records are generated by survival ‚Äî sleeping in a park is trespassing, standing on a street corner is loitering, asking for help is panhandling. The record makes employment impossible. Employment being impossible generates more survival behaviors. Those generate more records. The gap in employment grows, and hiring algorithms filter out anyone with a long gap, independent of the record entirely.

### Avenue 2: Ask a Stranger
A person can receive a sandwich by asking on the street, if they are "trusted" by the public and have no panhandling record when the ban is active.

The catch: trust is degraded by having a criminal record, and panhandling (the act of asking) is itself the crime that produces the record that produces the distrust. The first time you ask, you may succeed. The second time, you are flagged. After that, strangers will not help, and asking again is a new charge.

### Avenue 3: Apply for Government Aid
A person can receive a sandwich through government assistance, if they have a verified address, internet connectivity (services are online-only), and are housed.

The catch: a verified address requires being housed. Being housed requires resources. Resources require the sandwich. The aid that is supposed to break the cycle requires the stability that the cycle prevents.

## The 14 Catch-22 Systems

Each of the following is formally modeled as a named predicate in `final.frg`. Each one adds a new resource field to `Person` and a new policy lever to `Society`. Each one is triggered by poverty and produces a condition that deepens the trap. The `resourcesDegradation` predicate runs every tick, advancing each degradation one step closer to its terminal state.

### 1. The Credit Catch
You need a loan to pay off high-interest debt. High-interest debt destroys your credit score. A destroyed credit score bars you from low-interest loans ‚Äî the only loans that could let you pay off the high-interest debt. In the model: `debtLevel = HighInterestDebt` ‚Üí `creditScore = BadCredit` ‚Üí `creditCheckForLoans = Active` ‚Üí loan denied ‚Üí debt persists.

### 2. The Resume Gap
You need a job to explain why you are not working. Hiring algorithms filter out anyone who has been unemployed for too long ‚Äî "long-term unemployed" is treated as a disqualifying signal, not a circumstance. The gap grows longer by being filtered out, which makes the next cycle of filtering more aggressive. In the model: `resumeGap` advances from `ShortGap ‚Üí LongGap ‚Üí AlgorithmFiltered` each tick of unemployment. `AlgorithmFiltered` makes `jobAvenue` impossible regardless of record.

### 3. The Cleanliness Barrier
You need to be clean to get a job. You need a gym membership to shower. You need a bank account to get the membership. You need a job to get the bank account. In the model: `bankAccount = NoAccount` ‚Üí `gymMembershipRequired = Active` ‚Üí `hygiene` degrades from `Clean ‚Üí Unkempt ‚Üí Unsanitary` each tick ‚Üí `hygiene = Clean` is required for `jobAvenue`. The circle is formally closed.

### 4. The Legal Defense Loop
You need a lawyer to prove your innocence. Legal fees require selling assets. Selling assets leaves you unable to support your dependents. Inability to provide for dependents is itself a crime: `FailureToProvide`. In the model: `legalStatus = Charged` + `savings = Zero` + `mandatoryLegalFees = Active` ‚Üí `legalStatus' = AssetsSeized` ‚Üí `FailureToProvide in records'` ‚Üí new charge added to the record that already could not be defended.

### 5. The Insurance Paradox
You need health insurance to treat a chronic illness. The illness makes full-time work impossible. The loss of full-time work cancels employer-based insurance, which is the only available insurance. In the model: `healthStatus = ChronicIllness` + `insurance = Uninsured` ‚Üí `healthStatus' = Incapacitated`. Incapacitation removes any possibility of employment. `employerInsuranceOnly = Active` means unemployment = no insurance = illness worsens.

### 6. The Internet Essential
You need a web connection to apply for social services. The services provide the funds to pay the phone bill. No bill payment means no connection. In the model: `onlineServicesOnly = Active` means `aidAvenue` requires `connectivity = Connected`. `bankAccount = NoAccount` means the phone bill cannot be paid, so `connectivity' = Disconnected`. The application cannot be submitted.

### 7. The Transportation Sink
You need a car to reach the only affordable housing, which is far from work. The commute costs eat the savings that affordable rent was supposed to create. The car breaks down ‚Äî no savings to repair it ‚Äî and you lose both the job and the housing in the same tick. In the model: `housingFarFromWork = Active` + `transport = HasCar` + `savings = Zero` ‚Üí `transport' = BrokenCar`. `BrokenCar` ‚Üí `employment = Unemployed` and `status = Homeless` simultaneously.

### 8. The Expungement Wall
You need a clean record to get a high-paying job. You need roughly $2,000 in legal fees to expunge the record. Low-paying jobs ‚Äî the only ones available with a dirty record ‚Äî cannot generate $2,000 in savings. In the model: `recordClean = DirtyRecord` + `expungementFeeRequired = Active` ‚Üí `employment = Unemployed or Blacklisted`. `savings = Zero` ‚Üí `recordClean' = DirtyRecord` (cannot move to `ExpungementPending`). The fee is a locked door.

### 9. The Verification Spiral
You need a birth certificate to get an ID. You need an ID to request a birth certificate. If the family members who could vouch for you are gone ‚Äî dead, incarcerated, estranged ‚Äî there is no alternative path. In the model: `idStatus = CannotProveIdentity` + `birthCertRequired = Active` ‚Üí `label = NoAddress` (no ID means no residency proof) ‚Üí `not aidAvenue` and `not jobAvenue`. The spiral has no exit.

### 10. The Safety Deposit
You need a stable home to stop "survival spending" ‚Äî fast food instead of groceries, laundromats instead of home washing, convenience stores instead of bulk. Survival spending prevents saving for a security deposit. No deposit means no stable home. In the model: `status = Homeless` ‚Üí `savings` decreases every tick regardless of income. `securityDepositRequired = Active` ‚Üí no deposit means no lease. The deposit requires savings that homelessness prevents.

### 11. The Childcare Knot
You need a job to pay for daycare. You need daycare to attend a job interview without your child. Bringing your child to the interview marks you as "unreliable" ‚Äî and in the model, the child crying is formally charged as `DisorderlyConduct`, which then appears on your record. In the model: `childcare = NoChildcare` + `employment = Unemployed` + `hunger = Desperate` ‚Üí `childcare' = ChildAtInterview` ‚Üí `DisorderlyConduct in records'` ‚Üí record blocks job avenue ‚Üí cannot afford daycare.

### 12. The Banking Minimum
You need a bank account to avoid predatory check-cashing fees. The account requires a minimum balance. Low wages keep the balance below the threshold that triggers fees. The fees drain the account below minimum, converting it to `PreyedUpon` status. In the model: `bankAccount = BelowMinimum` + `bankMinimumBalance = Active` ‚Üí `bankAccount' = PreyedUpon`. `bankAccount = NoAccount` ‚Üí `CheckFraud in records'` (bounced check from using no-account financial services).

### 13. The Professional Wardrobe
You need "business casual" clothes to be considered for an office job. The clothes cost more than a week's food budget. Choosing food over clothes results in a "unprofessional" appearance that disqualifies you. In the model: `hunger = Desperate` + `savings = Zero` ‚Üí `wardrobe` degrades toward `Ragged`. `wardrobe = Ragged` + `dresscodeEnforced = Active` ‚Üí `not jobAvenue`. The dress code is a filter that was designed for people who already have resources.

### 14. The Address Verification
You need a utility bill in your name to prove residency for a lease. You need a lease to open a utility account. You need a residency proof to sign the lease. In the model: `status = Homeless` + `utilityBillForLease = Active` ‚Üí `label = NoAddress`. `label = NoAddress` ‚Üí `not aidAvenue`. The circle of documentation has no starting point for someone who has never had documentation.

## The Media Misreporting Mechanic

This is the formal heart of the creative argument. The model defines twelve distinct `StoryFrame` sigs ‚Äî types of news stories that Society's media can run. Several of them pool structurally different things as equivalent:

**`CrimeWave`** fires whenever any person has a survival infraction record (trespassing, loitering, panhandling, vagrancy, curfew violation). It is reported as a "rising crime" story alongside, and indistinguishable from, stories about murder and armed robbery.

**`CriminalElement`** fires whenever any person has any record of any type. It makes no distinction between a trespassing charge from sleeping in a park and a murder conviction. Employer background checks have the same behavior: the output is binary. There is a record, or there is not.

**`DebtCrisisFrame`** fires when anyone is in high-interest debt. It reports this as "irresponsible borrowing," ignoring that the debt was generated by predatory check-cashing fees that exist because the person had no bank account.

**`UnemployedLazy`** fires when anyone has a long employment gap. It reports this as "won't work," ignoring that hiring algorithms automatically filter out anyone with that gap regardless of their qualifications or effort.

**`PublicHealthThreat`** fires when anyone has unsanitary hygiene. It ignores that the hygiene degraded because the person had no bank account, no gym membership, and no shower access.

**`DrainOnSystem`** fires when an elder is homeless. It frames the person as a burden on taxpayers, ignoring that the system produced this outcome through 60-plus years of compounding catch-22s.

**`HumanInterest`** ‚Äî the sympathetic story ‚Äî can only run when none of the negative frames are active. In practice, given the model dynamics, it almost never fires. And when it does, its effect on sympathy is slow: `None ‚Üí Low ‚Üí Medium`. A single negative story in the next tick erases the gain. The ratchet goes one way.

## The Sympathy-Policy Feedback Loop

`publicSympathy` (one of `High, Medium, Low, None`) determines which laws are active or suspended. This creates the core feedback loop:

1. Poverty generates survival behaviors
2. Survival behaviors generate crimes
3. Crimes generate media stories
4. Media stories degrade sympathy
5. Low sympathy activates all restrictive laws
6. Restrictive laws generate more survival crimes
7. Go to step 2

The `policyDynamics` predicate encodes something important about structural barriers: even at `High` sympathy, laws like `creditCheckForLoans`, `algorithmHiring`, `mandatoryLegalFees`, `expungementFeeRequired`, `birthCertRequired`, `bankMinimumBalance`, and `securityDepositRequired` remain `Active`. These are not laws that legislatures pass in response to public mood. They are the architecture of private systems ‚Äî banks, landlords, courts, employers ‚Äî that do not respond to sympathy at all.

## The Birth-to-Death Lifecycle

`Person` has a `stage` field: `Infant ‚Üí Youth ‚Üí Adult ‚Üí Elder`. It advances every tick. The `init` predicate starts the person as an `Infant` with the worst plausible endowment: no ID, no bank account, no credit, no connectivity, no savings, no childcare, no address, no car, no insurance, no employment. This is not a person who made bad choices. This is a person who was born into a configuration the system was already designed to perpetuate.

By `Adult`, the compounding of degradation predicates means that nearly every resource field is at its worst terminal state. By `Elder`, the `DrainOnSystem` media frame fires, and the system that produced the outcome now frames it as the person's failure.

`neverFed[p]` is the predicate that says the person was never at any point in their life in possession of the sandwich. The UNSAT check `bornIntoTrapNeverFed` asks: is there any trace ‚Äî any possible sequence of steps ‚Äî in which a person born into the starting configuration eventually gets the sandwich? The solver's answer is no.

## The Test Suite

`final_tests.frg` is organized into 12 sections covering roughly 90 individual test cases. The structure:

**Sections 1‚Äì3** verify the model's basic plumbing: init state correctness, life stage transitions, the three original avenues and their blocking conditions.

**Section 4** tests all 14 catch-22 predicates individually ‚Äî both that they fire under the right conditions (`is sat`) and that they do not fire when conditions are not met (`is unsat`).

**Section 5** tests crime accumulation for all 12 crime types, including the key property that records are monotone: they only grow, never shrink.

**Section 6** tests resource degradation ‚Äî that credit degrades with debt and unemployment, that hygiene degrades without banking, that cars break with zero savings, etc.

**Section 7** tests the misreporting mechanics ‚Äî that survival crimes trigger `CrimeWave`, that any record triggers `CriminalElement`, that `HumanInterest` is blocked by any active negative frame, and that `PolicyDebate` runs in every tick forever (accomplishing nothing).

**Section 8** tests sympathy and policy dynamics, including the key asymmetry: human interest can slowly raise sympathy, but a single negative frame reverses the gain instantly, and structural barriers never relax regardless of sympathy level.

**Section 9** tests temporal properties using LTL operators: that the person eventually reaches `Desperate` hunger, that `AlgorithmFiltered` gap is inevitable, that `CriminalElement` eventually runs, that sympathy eventually collapses to `None`, that records are monotone over the full trace.

**Section 10** contains the core UNSAT proofs: that no avenue is open at init, that someone born into the trap cannot get the sandwich, that someone with all survival crimes and all checks active has zero open avenues, and that `deeplyTrapped` is a closed state.

**Section 11** tests counterfactual policy changes: which single-policy suspensions open which avenues, and crucially, which single-policy suspensions are insufficient on their own.

**Section 12** covers edge cases: all 12 crime types coexisting, all 12 media frames running simultaneously, all 20 policy fields suspended at once, etc.

## The Visualization (layout.cnd)

The `layout.cnd` file uses Cope and Drag (CnD), Forge's declarative layout language for Sterling. Load it in Sterling's Layout tab.

The visual language is intentional:

**Society floats above Person.** Power is spatial. The institution is literally overhead.

**Life stages form a horizontal timeline**, left to right. You can watch `Infant ‚Üí Youth ‚Üí Adult ‚Üí Elder` as a march across the screen.

**Crimes cluster below the person.** The burden accumulates at their feet. As you step through the trace, the cluster grows.

**Survival crimes are orange. Violent crimes are dark red.** The `CrimeWave` and `CriminalElement` media stories point to both colors with the same arrow. This is the misreporting mechanic made visible: two completely different types of thing, same story, same employer output.

**The 14 catch-22 resource fields are shown as inline attributes on Person**, not as edges. This keeps the diagram legible ‚Äî you can see 20+ fields at a glance without a web of crossing edges.

**Society's 20 policy fields are also inline attributes.** Watch them flip between `Active` (red) and `Suspended` (teal) as sympathy changes.

**The `possesses` edge is bright green and thick.** If the person ever gets the sandwich, it is unmissable. Its conspicuous absence across the full trace is the point.

**Temporal policy is set to `manual`**, meaning you step through the trace by hand in Sterling. The recommended narration:

- Step 0: Born. Clean slate. All laws active. No options.
- Step 1‚Äì2: Move to park. Trespassing charge accumulates. Resume gap starts.
- Step 3: Media runs `CrimeWave`. Sympathy was already `Low`, now activates `None` track.
- Step 4: Hygiene starts degrading. Debt accumulates from no banking.
- Step 5: All three avenues confirmed closed. `fullyTrapped` is now true.
- Steps 6+: Degradation continues. Nothing changes. The trace loops.

## Key Forge Concepts Used

This project uses most of the Temporal Forge feature set:

`#lang forge/temporal` enables lasso trace semantics ‚Äî every trace is infinite and loops back, which is exactly right for a model of a life.

`option max_tracelength 16` and `option min_tracelength 4` bound the trace. 16 steps is enough to see a full life cycle; 4 is the minimum to reach a meaningful trapped state.

`var` fields on `Person` and `Society` declare what changes over time. Non-`var` sigs (crime types, location names, status labels) are fixed ‚Äî they are the vocabulary, not the state.

`lone sig Sandwich {}` means zero or one sandwiches exist. This is the right cardinality for the model ‚Äî the sandwich is the scarce goal, and it either exists or it doesn't.

`always step` in the `trace` predicate means every transition in the infinite trace satisfies `step`. This is the standard Temporal Forge pattern for modeling a system that runs forever.

`eventually` and `always` are the two main LTL operators used in run blocks: `eventually some p.possesses` asks if there is any future state where the goal is reached; `always fullyTrapped[p]` asserts the trap never opens.

Priming (`'`) is used throughout the transition predicates to refer to next-state values: `p.records'` means the set of records in the next step. The monotonicity property (`p.records in p.records'`) is expressed and tested directly.

`is sat` / `is unsat` in `test expect` blocks are Forge's formal verification mode. `is unsat` is not a test that fails ‚Äî it is a proof that the named scenario is impossible under the model's constraints.

Named predicates (`jobTrap`, `addressTrap`, `creditCatch`, `wardrobeCatch`, etc.) make the model readable and reusable. Each catch-22 is a first-class named object that can be tested in isolation, composed into `deeplyTrapped`, and referenced in counterfactual run blocks.

## Open Questions

1. **Scarcity**: The model uses `lone Sandwich` ‚Äî at most one sandwich exists. Is this right, or should sandwiches be a pool that depletes? The current design makes the sandwich an abstract goal (stability, not food), which seems correct.

2. **Multiple persons**: The model supports `exactly N Person` in run bounds. With two people, you can model who gets helped and who does not, and whether the media story about one person affects the other's access. This is a meaningful extension.

3. **Policy change as an action**: Currently, policy changes are driven entirely by sympathy, which is driven by media. There is no action in the model for "a person organizes, sympathies shift, a law is suspended." Adding this would make the counterfactual reach goal richer.

4. **The UNSAT result interpretation**: The main theorem (`bornIntoTrapNeverFed`) is formally UNSAT, but the model has finite trace length. If `max_tracelength` were unbounded, would the result change? No ‚Äî because the `resourcesDegradation` predicate drives all resource fields monotonically toward their worst states. There is no recovery path in the model without external policy change.

## Related but Not Modeled

- Labels expiring over time (e.g., documentation becoming stale)
- Geographic specificity (distance to services, transit deserts)
- Scarcity of sandwiches (competition between trapped people)
- Real welfare policy names and dollar amounts
- Race, gender, and other identity factors that affect which catches trigger first
- Intergenerational transmission (one person's trap starting the next person's `init`)
